Reflection on Problem Solving Skills

Reflection Questions and Answers

1. How do you determine the most efficient approach when solving a complex problem?

I analyze the constraints of the problem, such as input size, performance requirements, and resources. Then, I evaluate multiple algorithms and techniques based on their time and space complexity. The approach with the best trade-off between efficiency and simplicity is chosen.

2. Reflect on a situation where you need to balance multiple conflicting constraints in a design. What approach did you take?

In a project optimizing traffic flow, I prioritized emergency vehicle routing over commuter delays. I used a weighted graph algorithm with dynamic edge weights to balance these constraints while maintaining overall traffic efficiency.

3. What criteria do you use to evaluate the effectiveness of a solution?

Effectiveness is evaluated based on the following criteria:

  • Time Complexity: How quickly the solution executes.
  • Space Complexity: The amount of memory used.
  • Scalability: The ability to handle larger datasets effectively.
  • Robustness: The capability to handle edge cases and unexpected inputs.
  • Applicability: Whether the solution can be applied to the real-world problem effectively.

4. How can you adapt an existing solution to address a new or unforeseen challenge?

Adapting a solution involves modifying its logic or parameters to meet new requirements. For instance, I modified Dijkstraâ€™s algorithm to handle dynamic edge weights, enabling real-time traffic routing updates.

5. What strategies do you use to identify patterns or structures in complex datasets or problems?

Identifying patterns involves using data visualization tools, clustering techniques, and statistical analysis. Decomposing the data into smaller subsets also helps detect patterns and relationships.

6. How do you decide when to prioritize simplicity over optimization in a solution?

Simplicity is prioritized during the early stages of problem-solving to ensure feasibility and understanding. Optimization is pursued later, especially when performance enhancements are needed for scalability or efficiency.

7. Reflect on how breaking down a problem into smaller components can help you approach it more effectively.

Breaking down a problem simplifies its complexity, making it easier to analyze and solve. For example, in graph traversal, separating the tasks of edge processing and vertex marking makes the algorithm modular and easier to debug.

8. Reflect on the trade-offs while choosing between different approaches to solve a problem.

Trade-offs often involve time complexity, space complexity, and implementation effort. For example, recursion simplifies code but may consume more memory compared to iterative methods. Choosing the right approach depends on the problem requirements and resource constraints.

9. How do you identify and address potential limitations or weaknesses in a proposed solution?

Rigorous testing, including edge cases and stress tests, helps identify weaknesses. Peer reviews and real-world simulations provide additional insights. Addressing limitations involves refining the algorithm or introducing auxiliary techniques to overcome specific challenges.

10. Reflect on how applying knowledge from one context can help you solve a problem in a different context.

Knowledge transfer is invaluable in problem-solving. For instance, graph algorithms used in social networks for friend recommendations can be adapted to logistics optimization for route planning. This cross-domain application often leads to innovative solutions.

11. How do you decide when to innovate versus relying on tried-and-tested solutions?

Innovation is necessary when facing novel problems where existing solutions do not suffice. For standard problems, tried-and-tested solutions are preferred as they save time and ensure reliability. The decision depends on the uniqueness of the problem and the associated risks.
